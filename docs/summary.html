Suppose that events A and B each have nonzero probability. Determine whether each statement is TRUE or FALSE.

  • When A and B are independent, it follows that P(A | not B) = P(not B).
  • The addition rule simplifies to P(A and B) = P(A) + P(B) when A and B are mutually exclusive.
  • The joint probability P(A and B) is equal to zero as long as A and B are independent.
  • If A and B are complementary, then P(A) = 1 − P(B).

Among the great selection of amusements and rides in a typical fair is a quick game of rolling a biased die. The distribution of the die’s faces is tabulated below.

face 1 2 3 4 5 6
prob 0.17 0.15 0.09 0.19 0.21 0.19

You have to shell out $3 to play the game. The mechanics are simple:

  • If you land on a face lower than 3, your payout is $12.
  • If you land on a face higher than 3, you pay the game operator $4.
  • Otherwise, your payout is $28.

On average, playing this game many times leads to a small positive gain of about $1 per play.

The game is fair because the gains and losses are balanced across the different outcomes.
Because more than half of the outcomes lead to a loss, the game must have a negative expected value.
The expected value can be estimated by looking mainly at the most likely outcome (face 5), since it contributes most to the average.

At the peak of operational pressure, the City of Austin Animal Shelter undertook a comprehensive review of its historical intake and outcome records. The goal was to better understand how early intake conditions might relate to eventual adoption outcomes.

For each animal, two binary variables were recorded:

stray: whether the animal was taken in as a stray (stray / not stray)
adopted: whether the animal was ultimately adopted (adopted / not adopted)

To explore the relationship between intake condition and outcome, the analyst computed: 

xtabs(~ stray + adopted, data = shelter) %>%
  prop.table(margin = 2) %>%
  round(2)
