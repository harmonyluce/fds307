2  Describing Data

2.1 Grammar of Graphics

2.2 Fundamental Plots

Distribution of Numerical Variables

You’ve likely seen class test scores summarized with a single number like the mean or median, or glanced at a table of grades and noticed that some values cluster near the top while others are scattered lower down. In many everyday situations—exam results, survey summaries, even sports statistics—this single-number summary is often treated as if it captures the entire story of the data. But it quietly leaves out something essential. Two classes, for example, can share the same average score and still look completely different in reality: one may have most students tightly grouped around the center, while another may contain a mix of very high and very low scores that balance out to the same mean. To understand these differences, we need to move beyond isolated summaries and look at the full structure of the data. This is where the idea of a distribution becomes central. The distribution of a numerical variable describes how all values are arranged across their range—where they tend to cluster, how widely they spread, and whether certain regions contain unusually high or low concentrations of values. Rather than reducing data to a single representative number, it allows us to see the data as an organized whole, where meaning comes from patterns of variation rather than individual observations.

To make these patterns visible, we turn first to graphical representations that reveal the distribution directly rather than describing it indirectly. Among the most fundamental of these are histograms and boxplots. A histogram provides a detailed view of how numerical values are distributed across their range, showing where observations concentrate and where they are sparse. A boxplot, in contrast, offers a more compressed summary of the same distribution, highlighting the median, the spread of the middle half of the data, and the presence of unusually high or low values. Although these two displays differ in how much detail they preserve, they serve the same essential purpose: to translate a list of numbers into a visible structure that can be interpreted at a glance. Together, they form the first step in moving from raw numerical data to meaningful understanding of its distributional shape.

Once the distribution of a numerical variable has been made visible through graphical tools, the next step is to describe its key features in a more compact and formal way. While histograms and boxplots allow us to see shape and structure directly, it is often useful to summarize these patterns using a small set of numerical measures that capture the essence of what we observe. This is where measures of center and spread become important. The mean and median provide two different ways of describing the typical value of a distribution: the mean reflects a balance point influenced by all observations, while the median identifies the central position of the data in a way that is less affected by extreme values. Alongside these measures of center, we also need ways to describe variability. The standard deviation captures the overall degree to which values deviate from the mean, while the interquartile range focuses specifically on the spread of the middle portion of the distribution. Together, these summaries do not replace the visual representation of a distribution; rather, they condense it, allowing us to compare and communicate its most important features more efficiently while still remaining grounded in the structure revealed by the plots.

Z-scores

While graphical displays and numerical summaries allow us to understand the overall shape, center, and spread of a distribution, they do not always answer a more focused question: where does a particular value stand relative to the rest of the data? A score of 80, for example, is not inherently high or low unless it is interpreted in the context of its distribution. In some datasets it may be exceptional, while in others it may be quite ordinary. To make such comparisons meaningful, we need a way to express values on a common scale that accounts for both the center of the distribution and its variability. This leads to the idea of standardization. A z-score measures how far a value lies from the mean of its distribution in units of standard deviation, allowing different observations—across different datasets or even different contexts—to be compared on a consistent scale. In this way, z-scores shift our focus from raw values to relative position within a distribution, completing the progression from describing data, to summarizing it, to interpreting individual observations within its structure.

Comparing Group Summaries

While numerical summaries allow us to condense and compare distributions of quantitative data, not all data behaves in the same way. Many important variables are categorical in nature, describing group membership rather than numerical magnitude—such as departments, regions, or types of outcomes. In these cases, the focus shifts from describing how values are spread along a number line to understanding how observations are allocated across distinct groups. This is where bar plots become essential. A bar plot translates categorical data into a visual form by displaying the frequency or proportion of observations within each category, making comparisons across groups immediately visible. Rather than summarizing shape or spread in a continuous sense, it emphasizes differences in magnitude between discrete categories. In this way, bar plots extend the same underlying idea of distribution to a different kind of data, allowing us to see how observations are distributed across groups rather than along a numerical scale.

Relationship Between Numerical Variables

As we move beyond the description of a single variable or the comparison of separate groups, the focus of data analysis shifts toward understanding how variables relate to one another. In many real-world contexts, data is not observed in isolation; instead, each observation contains multiple measurements that may be connected in meaningful ways. To explore these connections, we use graphical tools that allow us to see how one numerical variable behaves in relation to another. The most fundamental of these is the scatterplot, which represents each observation as a point in a two-dimensional space. This simple construction makes it possible to perceive patterns of association—whether variables tend to increase together, move in opposite directions, or show no clear relationship at all. In doing so, scatterplots extend the idea of distribution from a single dimension to a paired structure, where the focus is no longer on how values are distributed individually, but on how they co-vary.

2.2.1 Line Graph

In a related but distinct setting, we are often interested not only in relationships between variables, but in how a single variable evolves across an ordered structure such as time or sequence. In these cases, preserving order becomes essential, since the arrangement of observations carries meaning in itself. Line graphs are designed for this purpose. By connecting consecutive observations, they emphasize continuity and allow patterns such as trends, cycles, and gradual shifts to emerge clearly. Unlike scatterplots, where the order of points is irrelevant, line graphs rely on structure in the x-dimension to reveal how a variable changes over progression. Together, scatterplots and line graphs complete the visual framework of this chapter by extending analysis from distributions, to comparisons, and finally to relationships and change over order.
